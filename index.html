FSAE Rear Beam Wing
Formula SAE · Aerodynamics 📅 Oct 2025 – Dec 2025 🏎 Cal Poly Pomona FSAE ⏱ 6-week R&D
Goals

Design a rear beam wing to increase downforce for Cal Poly Pomona's Formula SAE vehicle while staying within the 76.5×38×20 cm size constraint. The goal was to evaluate whether a beam wing addition would be beneficial to the current aero package through a rigorous 6-week R&D cycle.

Design Concept
Airfoil Selection

Researched and selected NACA airfoil geometries optimized for high downforce at low Reynolds numbers typical of FSAE speeds. Multiple profiles were compared through iterative CFD runs before settling on the final geometry.

CFD Methodology

ANSYS Fluent and SolidWorks Flow Simulation were used to model pressure contours, velocity fields, and wake behavior. Simulations were run with the full car body to capture interference effects from the undertray and diffuser.

  • Achieved ~5 lb increase in downforce through beam wing and undertray improvements; identified wake interference as the primary limiting factor
  • Rearward center-of-pressure shift noted as a secondary design consideration
  • Determined addition of a beam wing would be incoherent with the overall goals and cost of the current aero package
  • Presented findings to 50+ team members to assess cost-benefit and system-level integration
What Was Learned
  • Wake interference is a critical limiting factor in multi-element wing design at FSAE scale
  • CFD workflow for iterative aero design: mesh sensitivity, convergence criteria, post-processing pressure and force data
  • How to structure and communicate a technical recommendation to a large engineering team
  • The importance of system-level thinking — an aerodynamic gain doesn't justify the cost and complexity if it conflicts with vehicle balance goals
Future Improvements
  • Explore multi-element wing configurations to increase downforce beyond the single-element limit
  • Integrate beam wing analysis with diffuser modeling for a full underbody aero package evaluation
  • Validate CFD results with physical testing using a load cell and pitot tube setup

Winch Sled
Mechanical Design 📅 Jan – Mar 2026 👥 Team Lead
Goals

Design and build a winch sled capable of pulling 4 circular 500g masses a height of 5ft and a horizontal distance of 30m in minimum time, operating under a strict $25 budget and powered by a 4V DC battery.

Design Concept
Pulley System

A 2:1 mechanical advantage pulley system was designed to double the effective force output of the motor, allowing a smaller and cheaper servo to pull the full load. The monorail carriage was built from 2×4 pine for rigidity and low friction.

Energy Storage

A flywheel was incorporated to store and release rotational energy, smoothing out peak current demands on the 4V battery and sustaining pull speed over the full 30m distance. Battery efficiency and servo performance were modeled together.

  • Mechanical advantage trade-offs: doubling force halves speed, so optimizing the ratio for both pulling force and time was critical
  • Battery discharge behavior under servo load and how to account for voltage sag in timing estimates
  • Team coordination and division of design tasks under a tight deadline and budget
Future Improvements

  • Explore higher voltage battery options for increased motor speed
  • Reduce friction on the monorail carriage with PTFE pads or roller bearings

Goals

Model the annual cost of running residential air conditioning in California using experimental data collected from a scaled insulated box. The goal was to predict how insulation quality, lux levels, and ambient temperature affect HVAC operating cost.

Design Concept
Data Acquisition

Vernier sensors were used to collect lux, surface temperature, and internal air temperature data from an insulated box model across multiple configurations. Over 1000 data points were collected and filtered for outliers before analysis.
